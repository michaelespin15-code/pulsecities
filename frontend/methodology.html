Methodology

How PulseCities Works

PulseCities is a free tool that shows displacement pressure in every NYC neighborhood using public data. The goal is simple: make it easy to see what is actually changing on your block before it is too late.

Everything below is the honest version of how it works. What data feeds the tool, how the score is calculated, what it can and cannot tell you.

What this is

A nightly pipeline that pulls public data from New York City, joins it together by Borough Block Lot (BBL), and produces a displacement risk score for every neighborhood in the city. You click a neighborhood, you see the score, the signals driving it, and what has been happening on the ground.

You can also search any landlord or LLC name to see their full NYC portfolio, including how many shell companies they use, what buildings they own, and what violations or evictions are connected to them.

What data feeds it

Every dataset is public. Nothing proprietary.

311 Complaints NYC Open Data · erm2-nwe9

Housing complaints, heat and hot water, illegal construction. Updated daily.

DOB Building Permits NYC Open Data · ipu4-2q9a

Filing date, permit type, owner name, contractor name. Alt-1 permits are the biggest displacement signal. Updated daily.

HPD Housing Violations NYC Open Data · wvxf-dwi5

Class A through C violations, including lead paint, no heat, structural hazards. The pipeline tracks violation density per building and velocity over time. Updated daily.

ACRIS Ownership Records NYC Open Data · bnx9-e6tj · 636b-3b5g

Every deed transfer in NYC since 1966. This is how PulseCities traces operators through shell LLCs to find their full portfolio. Updated weekly.

Eviction Filings NYC Open Data · 6z8x-wfk4

Marshal-executed evictions by address and date. Updated daily.

PLUTO Property Data NYC Dept of City Planning

80+ attributes per tax lot including zoning, units, year built, building class. Updated annually.

DOF Rolling Sales NYC Dept of Finance

Every property sale in NYC with price and date. Updated quarterly.

DHCR Rent Stabilization Counts NYC Rent Guidelines Board via NYCDB

Historical stabilized unit counts per building going back to 2007. Updated annually.

The displacement score

The score runs from 0 to 100. Higher numbers mean higher displacement pressure. Here is exactly how it is calculated.

Bonus signals

These adjust the composite score upward independent of the six weighted signals.

  • +8 pts Building appears on HPD Speculation Watch List
  • +4 pts Owner portfolio over 20 properties citywide
  • +3 pts HPD building registration lapsed

The methodology is grounded in real research

The weights are not invented. They are based on methodology from two sources that have validated these signals against actual displacement outcomes.

The first is the Association for Neighborhood and Housing Development Displacement Alert Project. ANHD has been scoring NYC buildings for displacement risk since 2016 using rent stabilized unit loss, tenant turnover, and sale prices. Their research shows these building-level signals are the strongest predictors of displacement outcomes.

The second is the NYC Equitable Development Data Explorer, built by the Department of City Planning and HPD. Their Displacement Risk Map combines population vulnerability, housing conditions, and market pressure at the neighborhood level.

PulseCities builds on both approaches — using building-level signals like ANHD while scoring at the neighborhood level like EDDE, and automating the operator network detection that both tools do manually or partially.

What PulseCities can tell you

  • ✓ Which neighborhoods are under the most displacement pressure right now
  • ✓ Who owns buildings in those neighborhoods and whether those owners are operating through shell LLCs
  • ✓ Which buildings have unresolved housing violations, active evictions, or recent ownership changes
  • ✓ Patterns like operators who buy buildings shortly after tenants are evicted, which require connecting multiple data sources

What PulseCities cannot tell you

  • ✕ Whether a specific landlord is breaking the law. Displacement pressure is not the same as illegal behavior. Many landlords operating in high-pressure neighborhoods are doing nothing wrong.
  • ✕ The future. The score describes current conditions. Whether a neighborhood's pressure translates to actual displacement depends on factors outside the data.
  • ✕ Every family's situation. A high-score neighborhood does not mean every tenant is at risk. A low-score neighborhood does not mean no one is being displaced.
The rent stabilization data is historical through 2023. More recent deregulation activity is not yet reflected. That gap will close as newer data becomes available. The assessment spike signal is currently dormant because it requires two consecutive years of DOF assessment history. Year one is being collected now.

How often data updates

Scrapers run every night at 2am UTC. Most datasets refresh daily. ACRIS data has a 2-week reporting lag from the city. Eviction data has a 2 to 4 week reporting lag. The site shows the timestamp of the most recent successful scraper run at the bottom of every page.
